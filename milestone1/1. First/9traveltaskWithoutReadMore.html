Living in Bangladesh, or BD as we call it, is like having a front-row seat to a vibrant tapestry of culture, nature, and history. I'm based in Dhaka, where the city-s hustle never stops, but every so often, I crave an escape to explore the gems of my country. This is the story of a three-day trip that reminded me why I love calling BD home.

Day 1: Into the Wild - The Sundarbans I kicked off my journey with a road trip from Dhaka to the Sundarbans, the world's largest mangrove forest. It's a UNESCO World Heritage site, and as a Bangladeshi, I feel a deep connection to this place, where the Ganges, Brahmaputra, and Meghna rivers meet. At Mongla Port, I hopped onto a wooden boat, the air thick with the salty scent of the delta and the hum of nature all around. Cruising through the narrow creeks, I spotted monkeys leaping through the trees and even caught sight of a tiger's paw print on the muddy bank - a thrilling moment, though the Royal Bengal Tiger stayed elusive. That night, I stayed at a simple eco-resort, savoring fresh hilsa curry while chatting with local fishermen about their lives. Moments like these make me realize how intertwined we are with nature here, though it's a bittersweet reminder of the environmental challenges we face.

Day 2: Chasing Waves at Cox's Bazar Next, I headed to Cox's Bazar, home to the world's longest unbroken beach, stretching 120 kilometers along the Bay of Bengal. The bus ride from the Sundarbans was a treat, with views of emerald paddy fields and tiny fishing villages that feel like snapshots of rural BD. When I reached the beach, the sound of waves crashing was like music to my ears. I took my friends to Himchari National Park, where waterfalls spill into the sea, and we gorged on street food - crispy singara and spicy fuchka that burst with flavor. Watching the sunset paint the sky in shades of orange and pink felt like a gift. Later, I walked along the shore and met some Rohingya families, whose resilience in the face of hardship left a deep impression. It's moments like these that show BD's heart.

Day 3: Back to Dhaka via Lalbagh Fort On the final day, I made my way back to Dhaka, stopping at Lalbagh Fort in the old city. This 17th-century Mughal fortress, started by Prince Muhammad Azam, is a piece of history I never tire of visiting. Walking through its red-brick corridors and quiet gardens, I felt a sense of pride in our heritage, even if the fort was never finished. By evening, I was back in Dhaka, where the chaos of rickshaws and street vendors greeted me like an old friend. After the calm of the past few days, the city's energy was a jolt, but it's part of what makes BD so alive. This trip was a whirlwind of nature, culture, and connection, and it reminded me why I cherish living here.
